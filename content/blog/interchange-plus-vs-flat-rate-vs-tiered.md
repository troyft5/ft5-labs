If you've ever tried to compare two processor quotes, you've hit the same wall every merchant does: one quote is flat rate, one is interchange-plus, and a third throws tiers at you. They're structurally incomparable — which is exactly why processors use all three.

Here's how each one actually works, who benefits from each, and where each model costs you money.

---

## Flat Rate Pricing

### How it works

You pay a single rate on every transaction, regardless of card type or entry method. Square, Stripe, and PayPal popularized this model.

**Typical rates:**
- Card-present: 2.6% + $0.10
- Card-not-present: 2.9% + $0.30
- Keyed: 3.5% + $0.15

### Who benefits

Flat rate is genuinely good for:
- Businesses processing **under $10,000/month** (predictability outweighs cost)
- Pop-up vendors, seasonal businesses, or side projects
- Businesses using a single platform's integrated tools (Square ecosystem, for example)

### The hidden cost

On a debit card, the interchange is about 0.05% + $0.21. Square charges you 2.6% + $0.10. The difference goes to Square.

At $50,000/month with a strong debit card mix, you could be paying 40–60% more than interchange-plus for the same volume. That "simplicity premium" gets expensive fast.

---

## Tiered Pricing

### How it works

Your transactions are sorted into buckets: **Qualified**, **Mid-Qualified**, and **Non-Qualified**. The processor sets rates on each tier and controls which transactions fall where.

**Example:**
- Qualified: 1.79% (basic debit and credit)
- Mid-Qualified: 2.39% (rewards cards, some card-not-present)
- Non-Qualified: 3.25% (corporate cards, manual key entry)

### The problem

You have zero visibility and zero control into how transactions are bucketed. Processors routinely downgrade cards to mid-qual or non-qual without explanation, increasing your cost. The "qualified" rate in the brochure isn't what most of your transactions actually get billed at.

**Tiered pricing is almost universally the most expensive model for volume merchants.** It was designed to be opaque — and it is.

### Who uses it

Smaller ISOs and legacy processors default to tiered because the margins are highest. If you're on tiered pricing and processing over $30,000/month, you should request an interchange-plus re-pricing immediately.

## Interchange Plus (IC+)

### How it works

You pay the actual interchange rate set by Visa/Mastercard/Discover, plus a fixed processor markup.

**Example:** IC + 0.25% + $0.10 per transaction

If a customer uses a Visa Signature Preferred rewards card (IC rate: 2.10% + $0.10), your total cost is:
**2.35% + $0.20**

If a standard debit card runs (IC rate: 0.05% + $0.22), your cost is:
**0.30% + $0.32**

### Why it's better for most merchants

1. **Full transparency** — Your statement shows exactly what interchange was, and what the markup is. Nothing is hidden.
2. **Lower average effective rate** for merchants processing over $15,000/month
3. **Card-type-appropriate pricing** — debit costs what debit should cost
4. **Negotiable** — the markup is the only variable, and it can be compressed

### The one caveat

IC+ statements are harder to read. The line items multiply quickly. That complexity is why merchants sometimes prefer tiered or flat — but it's a false trade. Confusion is not worth overpaying.

## How to Know What You're On

Look at your statement. If you see:
- One or two rates applied uniformly → flat rate
- Three tiers labeled "Qualified / MQ / NQ" → tiered
- Multiple interchange category names with explicit markups → IC+
